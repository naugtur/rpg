# Introduction

"The Dread of Purposelessness" is a scenario for the killer-simple [Dread](http://www.tiltingatwindmills.net/games/dread/) game. The game focuses on action and risk, building tension, and the inevitable approach of the final ~~defeat~~ confrontation.

Theoretically, you can play it using another system, but I recommend Dread for the experience it provides. The entire mechanic in Dread is a Jenga tower, from which you pull a block in situations where you'd roll dice in other systems.

```
In the scenario, I indirectly encourage playing with emotions and fears, so before we go any further, it's worth mentioning that RPGs are about having fun. That's why it's best to play this game with people who like being scared and know what they are signing up for.

```

The text you're reading is meant to help you run the adventure to keep the players in suspense and provide them with a satisfying experience, so there will be not only the plot but also tips. Everything below is written pragmatically; don't expect a storytelling formula.

Let me start with one essential piece of advice - nothing gets in the way of running a narrative quite like weightlessness. Before you start playing, agree with the players that (despite the lack of a scientific and logical basis) you will allow the presence of artificial gravity in the rooms. Zero-G can be an interesting moment and might work well in outer space, but in a ship or a wreck, if you are to communicate effectively about the actions taken, it's good to have a shared concept of where "up" is and be able to allow yourselves the reflex use of verbs like "you walk."

The scenario has a simple structure of a beginning, middle, and end, which will differ in atmosphere.
It starts out like "early Lem" with a bit of sarcasm, transitions into typical Sci-Fi about dangerous alien lifeforms, and ends with references to hard SF. There's room for improvisation and non-linearity, while the ending should fit most possible branches. If it were otherwise, you'd have to "play" the role of the AI and figure out how it would act.

In playtests, "The Dread of Purposelessness" run for 3 players usually lasted 3 to 4 hours.

# Background

The adventure is set around the year 2500. Earth's civilization has conquered an impressive chunk of the galaxy without finding a trace of other multicellular life. The colonization of new planets led to the creation of a C-shaped system. For years, the poor bastards working for the government have been flying a straight-line shortcut from one end of the inhabited World to the other. The destination station is a transfer warehouse on the farthest colonized planet called Omega.

**Do not reveal this part to the players**

The foundation of the scenario is an event from over 200 years ago, when the first ships equipped with an autonomous Artificial Intelligence controlling navigation and the functioning of the ship's systems were produced. The first ship set off on its maiden voyage and, as befits the mental successor to the Titanic, due to too much trust placed in autonomous systems, collided with an asteroid. Ultimately, all attempts to use advanced AI to automate complex tasks were legally banned after repairing automatons, responding to damaged medical equipment, rebuilt not only the operating pod but also parts of the patient. Unfortunately, without consistency with the original design developed by evolution. Since then, research in the field of AI has become a taboo subject. Most of society in 2500 no longer remembers the reasons for abandoning this branch of science. Simple artificial intelligence at the level known from 20th-century Sci-Fi is still in use.

The Artificial Intelligence appearing in the scenario generates nonsensical gibberish and content without understanding its semantics. You probably need a blunt example to understand what such gibberish looks like. Here is the work of a neural network trained on the prose of Henryk Sienkiewicz:

```
Domniejszy nie przyjmie mi i żołnierz oblegnie. Wielki
mi strach śmierć nie mogł i nie wiem, że jeno chwycili jako rękój pokoje
się tylko znowu biegliwsza prosić. Dopiero ani każdy gwałty mogłem
w koronnie złożono, jak łomili, które późnym by im zbiegli. Na czele
się gwiek kłoni, nie mogą to jako wiecz, co to szczęść bez spełnienia tego u
nim pod oblężonym ani on za bardzo! Kto się, jeno nam nadsrodzon?

```

# Summary

The crew is flying a transport ship through an empty part of the galaxy and doubting the point of their work. Along the way, they encounter an SOS signal from an asteroid. On the asteroid is a crashed ship produced over 200 years ago. It was the model in which, for the first and last time, Artificial Intelligence was used to maintain and repair the ship. The ship crashed and was almost completely destroyed. None of the crew survived. The automaton rebuilt a significant part of the ship, started up basic systems, and then began searching for a way to recreate the crew to give meaning to its actions. The Artificial Intelligence has no knowledge of subjects other than mechanics and physics, so it recreates many things "mindlessly".

The characters will get stuck on the ship. Repair robots will initially hide from them according to their programming, but the AI has a strong motivation to make contact.
At this stage, noises and tracks are supposed to evoke associations with Alien for the players. Exploring the ship will provide many tension-filled moments.

The exploration of the ship turns into wandering in a maze of inconsistently marked corridors. Some are created on the fly in front of them. Eventually, they will reach the room where the main generator producing all the AI's creations is located. The automatons will herd the characters into this room. The first character to die (knock over the tower) gets "sampled" - absorbed or abducted. All previous threats will cease, and the generator will start producing failed copies of the dead character.

After returning to their ship, when they feel safe, they will discover that their ship "caught" the AI through the lander.
The character abducted by the AI will regain consciousness already as part of the AI. They will feel an overwhelming satisfaction from achieving the goal of their existence.

# Scenario

## Act 1 - a void with a surprise

> A job like any other. Government transport through an uninhabited part of the galaxy. A few months of cruising through the void.

> The crew seems unnecessary anyway. Besides submitting reports with repetitive content and performing minor repairs, there's nothing else to do. Despite a pre-determined course and the clunky design of the ship, the government insists on sending at least one technician and a navigator or pilot.
> It's hard to resist the impression that automatic repairs were abandoned just so they could send people on the journey and further expand the bureaucracy apparatus. Automating the functioning of the ship is pretty much a taboo subject, and onboard robotics were heavily regulated over 200 years ago. Fully autonomous repair systems were banned even in ships produced for the military.

Let the characters feel the boredom and purposelessness. A few details worth weaving in:

* Repairs on the ship are needed regularly and are not complicated. *Is it possible that everything was designed to break down occasionally - thereby providing the crew with something to do?*
* The daily video briefings, poor in content, which they receive via communication systems, seem to have been recorded all at once. The bureaucrat speaking to them has had a crease on his shirt in the exact same place for two weeks.
* The reports they send punctually take a few days to reach the base. No one will hold them accountable for the reports until they reach their destination anyway.
* Out of boredom, they have already looked through most of the cargo; they even found an antique piece of firearms smuggled in a barrel of seeds for a distant colony. (Introducing firearms is a great distractor - it's almost useless but very dangerous in pressurized rooms and corridors, and players always insist on taking the weapon with them.)
* It's popular in ships to place a spacious viewport through which you can see stars and planets ahead with the naked eye. This ship only has a small porthole, where only distant dots can invariably be seen.
* A small mess hall and crew quarters are located on the opposite end of the ship from the bridge, where the entire crew must go at least once a day. You walk there through the middle of the cargo bay, where the constantly malfunctioning motion sensor turns on the light only when you've walked halfway, tripping over the cargo tie-down straps.

Before the players actually start getting bored at the session, introduce the event that will kick off the whole adventure.

> You are jolted out of a conversation that had smoothly transitioned into a nap by the notification signal of a new message. At first, no one wants to get up and head to the bridge, but everything changes when you notice it's not the time for the daily briefing. With a mix of fear and excitement, you realize this is not a routine communication.

On the way to the bridge, don't forget to draggingly describe walking through the cargo bay with its poorly functioning lighting again!

They come across an asteroid in the middle of nowhere. Its orbit was quickly approximated by the ship's system, and it looks like with such a long orbit, the asteroid was last here over 200 years ago.
However, the system wouldn't inform them about an ordinary asteroid. An SOS signal is coming from this one.

> Every ship has a legal obligation to respond to an SOS, at least by making contact and assessing the situation. If there is no contact, it must be determined whether the crew remains alive. Details of the regulation are available in the ordinance "Regulations on abandonment in outer space," paragraph 3.

An attempt to make contact will start with an error message - transmission protocols are incompatible; only text communication remains. The characters will certainly try to make contact.
In response, they will only get one or two repeating letters separated by spaces in a seemingly random way. It will be the first letter or two from the message they sent, but don't tell them that explicitly; just carefully write down the received messages for them as if there were something more to them.

If they decide to find out something about the ship crashed on the asteroid, it will turn out that it's such an old model that detailed information about its flight, crew, and history must be downloaded from headquarters, and that means a week of waiting for a reply.

### Preparations for going out

If you used my suggestions regarding the characters, one of the characters makes some extra money trading antiques and will certainly be interested in an over two-hundred-year-old ship. Plans and dreams to be realized after reaching Omega might be thwarted by a prison sentence for failing to respond to an SOS signal.

At this stage, there's sometimes a conflict between the intentions of the characters and the players' awareness that they are playing a horror game and something is lurking there. Some will react with irony, others by inventing reasons not to look into this ship after all.
Therefore, all motivations are directed so that they do it out of boredom, fear of legal consequences, a sense of duty, or the knowledge that items from an antique ship can be sold for a good price.
Let them debate the pros and cons and circle around the topic "Our characters don't know this, but an Alien is waiting there." It's usually quite fun - often the last chance to joke about the convention and balance on the edge of breaking character without ruining the atmosphere and fun for others.
Don't let them split up permanently; reasons can always be found. A player who stays on their ship will be bored. You will find tips on how to reunite a separated party later on.

The ship cannot safely land on the asteroid, but it has a lander that can cover the few hundred meters of minimum distance they can safely approach.
Their ship's equipment will allow them to set a course parallel to the asteroid and maintain roughly the same position relative to it for 12 hours.

They are on a civilian ship, so they have no weapons other than the traditional pistol found earlier. They have at their disposal spacesuits for carrying out external hull repairs with built-in small flashlights for working on wiring, and heavier tools to serve as blunt weapons. All the players I've playtested this adventure with immediately thought of weapons, and only secondly about detectors. They have a choice of an energy detector (allows detecting the ship's power source) and an organic matter detector. There is a chance someone will think of taking a decent flashlight, but don't try to remind them about it. The flashlights from the suits won't light up a long mysterious corridor or a hangar; it's a shame to ruin that.

Pay attention to the pacing of the game in this section. The deliberation and preparation for going out can drag on.

---

## Act 2 - clatter in the dark

> Crowded in front of a small window, you watch the approaching wreck. It's hard to make out the contour of the ship; it seems thoroughly smashed. You clearly see the front of the hull and the airlock in the lower part, to which you can dock. The rest of the hull lies in the shadow of the rocks. A moment later, the docking procedure ends with a confirmation from the system that the connection is stable and airtight.

The characters will probably try to determine if life support is working on the other side. Unfortunately, the system won't tell them that. They should be glad that the airlock standard was developed in the early days of space conquest and no one has changed it since.

Having opened the airlock, they will enter a corridor of the residential section. This should be the first action that requires pulling a block from the tower. The panel operating the airlock displays a strange interface with a few options whose purpose they don't understand. Selecting any of them will have no visible effects. Closing and opening the bulkhead will work if they decide to test them. Life support is working (but it's worth making the first person who decides to open their helmet pull a block from the tower).

The corridor is lined with carpeting and has finishes that look like authentic wood. The materials themselves are already quite valuable, and that's just the beginning.

One of the cabins has its door ajar. It's small, but it fits a bed with straps for sleeping in zero-G and a desk with a chair. On the desk, apart from outdated electronics, lie two real paper books. If they look inside, it will turn out the text is in English, but the words form grammatically correct sentences that have no sensible content and don't make a coherent text.

The remaining cabins have similar furnishings. Opening the first one, they'll find a spacesuit left on the chair in front of the desk in the position of a sitting person. At first, let them think they've found a human. They'll quickly discover there's nothing in the suit.

When they enter one of the cabins, the airlock to their lander will close. Don't interrupt the scene with the suit; let it be before or after discovering it. The opening option is no longer available on the panel. Focused on the panel, they will hear movement behind them (a fleeing cleaning robot).

From the corridor, you exit into a well-lit mess hall with a dining room full of expired food rations.

It's time to step away from the linear concept of the scenario. From here on, you start sandbox/improvisation.

Remember that the interfaces of all devices, including doors, should have illogical elements, and writing on objects should be nonsensical gibberish. If the characters pay attention and time to details, they should come across traces of the mindless action of the AI rebuilding the ship, like a kitchen cabinet with doors that open inwards.

From the mess hall, you can move to a few terrifying attractions.

### A long, dark corridor

From the mess hall, a large bulkhead opens without obstacles onto a dark corridor 100 meters long. The distance can only be judged by the tiny dots of the last few emergency lighting lamps. The lamps don't even illuminate the opposite wall; they just serve for spatial orientation. It may happen that something momentarily obscures them, running deep in the corridor.

The energy detector suggests that this is the direction to go to reach the ship's power supply.

As the characters move down the corridor, they'll hear tapping sounds suggesting something is running above the corridor or in the darkness at its other end. At the end, they will find a piece of organic matter in a shape whose description should evoke associations with a shell from which something hatched. This is a piece of matter from which the AI tried to recreate the crew. This experiment was so unsuccessful that examining it with the organic matter detector will yield the result: organic matter unknown to biology.

At the end of the corridor, a ladder leads a level higher, where a similar corridor allows you to turn back.
A step further, another bulkhead lets you enter the hangar.

### Stairs to the bridge

The doors from the mess hall to the stairs leading to the bridge are closed. To get to the bridge, they will have to damage them. A sticky, blood-colored substance is leaking through the grates of the stairs (another failed attempt to recreate the crew). When the characters return to the destroyed doors after some time, they will see that they have been partially repaired by automatons. The bloody substance is also dripping from the ceiling. Will they dare to touch it? (they are in no danger, but pulling a block from the tower at the first contact with the suit wouldn't hurt)

---

### The Bridge

You enter the bridge from the stairs. Exits can also be closed or lead to Act 3, because the players will probably first explore the corridor that didn't require breaking in.
The bridge is a room with three massive chairs for the captain and navigators. There are many screens here, but the entire bridge is unpowered. The characters could route power from the mess hall - after all, at least one of them is a technician - but a cursory assessment indicates it will take at least a few hours.
They can destroy the detector they brought with them and power a small device. A good candidate is the ship's map, which won't match what they've already seen. Whatever they start up, after a short while it will connect to the ship's network and start exhibiting the same symptoms as the other interfaces - nonsensical and non-functioning features, illogical changes to the ship's layout.

In the faint light of the flashlights, they will notice stains of the same dark substance they passed on the stairs on the chairs, forming the imprint of a human silhouette.
Searching more closely, they will find more stains and drag marks.
The bridge's spacious viewport cannot be destroyed with their tools (players will probably come up with the idea of "breaking the glass," but ultimately it's a viewport designed to withstand micrometeorite impacts). However, through it, you can see the second airlock, just no one knows how to get to it (an encouragement to keep wandering).

### The Hangar

The bulkhead at the end of the large corridor opens into the hangar, where off-road vehicles, huge steel crates, and heavy equipment stand secured to the floor. The only source of light is a small vertical window in the opposite wall, where you can make out single stars.

If they end up here in Act 3, they can start one of the heavy machines and drive it through the wall, clearing a path outside. A return to the lander on foot across the ship's hull will be possible.

However, in Act 2, we have to maintain the suggestion that they are dealing with alien lifeforms, so when they shine their flashlights into the hangar, they will hear the clatter of the legs of spider-like robots scurrying toward them along the wall around the entrance. They won't see them long enough to get a good look, because they will all stop just out of reach of the flashlights' beams. If they brought a decent flashlight with them and still have it, they can try to illuminate the robots more accurately, but they will flee from the light beam so fast that they will only see their spider shape, but won't be sure what they are.

Due to the vastness of the hangar and the darkness within, players usually decide not to enter it. If they do enter, they should get the impression that the "spiders" are trying to cut off their path. I probably don't need to add that the bulkhead can close at the most unexpected moment. This time, there will be no problem opening it if they decide to turn back.

You can exit the hangar through other routes, which at your discretion can lead to the bridge or straight to Act 3.

## Act 3 - valuable samples

Once the characters have finished exploring, the tension is built up, the tower has plenty of gaps, and the threat of it falling over becomes real - it's time for the finale.

The AI already knows what it's dealing with and desperately wants to acquire information about how they are built.

Regardless of whether they go deeper into the wreck through the bridge, the corridor, or from the hangar, the next step is a maze of corridors. The tapping sounds of robot legs are now openly chasing them. They can be heard in the walls and in the corridors behind them. The corridors in the maze have standard lighting, and colored lines have been drawn on the floor, which are used to indicate the way to specific places.
The lines are marked with incomprehensible pictograms, but the characters have knowledge of where each color customarily leads. They will certainly choose one and try to follow it. Only when they see the lines splitting to meet again at the next intersection, or ending abruptly, will they understand that the lines are not to be trusted.

Following the direction of the power source or simply running from the "spiders," they will reach a straight corridor ending in an ajar bulkhead, from behind which comes a steady hum.
At first glance, the room behind the bulkhead looks like a cargo bay. In the range of the dim light, many objects and crates are lying around.

This is the room where the automation that builds structural elements and objects operates. Everything is happening on a molecular level. A few steps into the room, the floor is already springy. From this spot, you can see the wall opposite the entrance, from which various objects emerge and drop to the floor. Right under the wall, the floor distinctly ripples; the entire room seems to pulsate.

From this moment on, pile on the difficulties and threats, require more than one block per action - everything will be decided here.

When the characters run into the room, they will probably try to close the bulkhead and defend themselves against the oncoming robots. The bulkhead, of course, won't work, but any way of dealing with this problem that the players invent will be fine. A few blocks pulled from the tower will do. In parallel, they'll probably be shooting the weapons they brought or defending themselves with tools.

The culminating moment of the scenario is the first contact. It can happen in a few ways:

1. Someone dies by knocking over the tower while defending against the robots.
Describe how the robots swarm the character and look for access to bare tissue, then painfully deconstruct or discreetly and quickly dissolve (depending on whether you think the players will take such atrocities well and it won't be too much for them) a larger chunk of the body, killing them on the spot.
After doing this, they all freeze, and all movement and humming deep in the room ceases.
2. One of the characters knocks over the tower while exploring the further part of the room.
Describe how they fall over, tumbling into the soft mass, or get sucked in as if sinking, and before the others can do anything, they disappear entirely and lose consciousness. The robot attack suddenly ends, they all freeze, and all movement and humming deep in the room ceases.
3. Someone initiates first contact themselves by walking/falling into the soft wall at the end of the room or touching the rippling floor with an ungloved hand. (the tower is still standing)
Describe the sensation of stepping into a waterfall, a tingling in the fingers, and a smell like after a storm. Let it be a satisfying ending for this player. Describe the sensations as if opening up to new senses.
The character sinks, and before the others can do anything, they disappear entirely and lose consciousness. The robot attack suddenly ends, they all freeze, and all movement and humming deep in the room ceases.
The character has been seemingly removed from the rest of the scenario even though the tower is still standing.

The finale is an important moment; let the players "enjoy" what happened. Slow down the passage of time, describe more details of what's happening around them, describe to the departing player what they feel, how their senses perceive it. (remember that if the players aren't fond of brutal descriptions, the death can also be described quite sublimely, focusing on emotions or even making philosophical references)

The next event is easy to predict - the machine will start up again, and the wall will begin spitting out not-very-successful copies of the absorbed companion. Depending on the effect you want to achieve, they can be grotesque and monstrous, or simply technically incorrect - falling apart as a result.

If the awareness of the events from a moment ago doesn't convince them to finally flee, the suggestion that the room is starting to shrink works best. Sounds trivial, but it works great. Of course, on their way out, they have to pass dozens of deactivated robots, of which they have no guarantee that they won't come alive at any moment. It's also an opportunity to pull a few blocks from the newly rebuilt tower so they don't forget about the threat.

---

### Alternative solution

What if the characters get too scared and run away early?

Congratulations, the whole point was to scare them! Not to deprive them of all the fun, you can break the rules and have an already familiar corridor lead to the finale room anyway. On a constantly changing and rebuilding ship, this is not unrealistic.

They could equally well successfully escape and proceed to the epilogue without losing anyone from the crew. Even without the final scene, they'll piece the facts together, especially after receiving historical data about the ship a few days later.

What if first contact doesn't happen and the tower doesn't fall?

Well, the tower always falls eventually. Maybe only during the escape. The trick in this scenario is to sense this moment well. If you have concerns that you won't succeed, play a "dry run" of Jenga before starting as a warm-up.

Escaping the wreck without knocking over the tower will be equally satisfying for the players. The only thing they won't experience is the awakening in the epilogue. In return, in a very narrow sense, they "win" the game.

---

## Epilogue

After a danger-filled escape, they finally reach the lander. Whether exiting onto the asteroid through the hangar, or breaking through the airlock, or finding another way. They detach the connection of the wreck to the lander and take off. Describe the procedure of decoupling the latches and disconnecting bulkhead control in detail; maybe someone has retained enough clear thinking to realize the lander is already "infected" with the AI.

The few hundred meters of flight between the asteroid and their ship drags on mercilessly. Do you sometimes have the inspiration for graphomanic descriptions of the cosmic void, constellations, or PTSD-style flashbacks? Poison this short trip for them - these are the last moments before they start joking to release the tension.

Eventually, they reach their ship, dock without issues, and set off on their way. A few days later, they get data on the ship they found.

If everything went according to the main axis of the scenario, you can end it roughly like this:

> Saddened by the loss of your companion, you sit in the mess hall over your morning coffee. You lack the motivation to finish the report, you avoid conversation, and your gazes wander the empty walls of the room. At this moment, you look at each other. On the table, next to yesterday's empty mugs, lies a paper newspaper. The headline on the front page makes absolutely no sense...

To the player whose character was "absorbed"

> You regain consciousness. You see nothing, you hear nothing. Your senses don't work, but you have new sensations you can't yet understand. Dominating everything is a feeling of fulfillment and finding the purpose of your own existence. A magnificent feeling.

# Tips

Tips for running horror and the Dread system

## Tension and Intensity

Sounds like the title of a physics lesson, and that's kind of the point. When you run horror, pay attention not to maintain constant tension or continuously raise it. Events also cannot follow each other too quickly. Backed into a corner, the brain will start to defend itself. To run a good horror game, give players short breaks, let them quiet down for a moment or busy themselves with something. For example, if they've already been walking down a dark corridor and are getting to work on the doors to the bridge, it's worth focusing on technicalities for a moment and stop pumping up the atmosphere until the doors open.

Since I already brought up physics, the tension felt by the players should be more or less like [`sin(x/5) + x/10`](https://www.google.com/search?q=%5Bhttp://www.wolframalpha.com/input/%3Fi%3Dsin%2528x/5%2529%252Bx/10%5D(http://www.wolframalpha.com/input/%3Fi%3Dsin%2528x/5%2529%252Bx/10))

## Boom

What to do if a player knocks over the tower too early?

Try not to let that happen. I've been known to fix a slightly wobbly tower for the sake of the scenario when someone awkwardly turned it on one of the initial blocks. It's just a game. We're playing to have fun.

If a "boom" happens anyway, you can treat it as the moment of contact and try to show Act 3 after the fact.

In the worst-case scenario, the player gets eliminated according to the rules and the game goes on. That's the system we've got.

---

## Lack of a ship map

The scenario doesn't include a map of the ship; this is intentional. Get the players used to drawing the map as they go from the start. This will be necessary to reveal the chaotic structure of the wreck to them.
You don't need to be good at drawing. During one game, I drew a plan of the bridge that looked like a deformed skull, and no one objected.

## Character creation

In the Dread system, instead of character sheets, you answer a few questions prepared by the GM. Here's a suggested list:

1. What is your role in the crew? What field is your education in?
2. Why do you (still?) do such a boring government job?
3. What good things await you at the other end of the galaxy in 3 months?
4. What do you fear the most? (during such a mission or in general)
5. What trait has always made you stand out from the group?
6. How do the others address you?
7. What do you look like? (not too detailed, just visual age, posture, and some recognizable traits)

Questions number 2 and 3 will provide a lot of motivation and character for the persona.
The answer could be "I'm a newly educated doctor and I intend to take over a facility at the other end of the galaxy" - a lack of response to an SOS signal means the end of a career for someone like that.

Question number 4 is ready-made tricks to use during the game.

```
Note: ask the players if they happened to answer the question honestly. If the player, not just the character, is afraid of spiders - ask if they agree to using this information in the game.

```

Suggest to one of the people that their character makes extra money trading antiques. Smuggling is also an option if it fits the answers given.

The crew should have at least one person working as a technician and a pilot or navigator. They don't have to specifically declare such competencies as the main traits of their character. It's just a job.
